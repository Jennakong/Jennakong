Why Do We Need More Clothes?

On value, waste, and what it means to make something that lasts.

"Why do we need more clothes?" That was the question a tutor at the Jimmy Choo Fashion Academy asked me during a conversation we were having. I knew he was trying to pick my brain a little, but my answer came almost immediately.

"We don't."

And I meant it.

We don't need more clothes in this world. There is already so much, too much, even. But then I caught myself and said something else:

"If we must make clothes, then what value are we adding?"

That's really the question. Because fashion, for me, has never just been about making something new. It's about meaning. It's about what that piece does for someone. Does it bring joy? Does it empower them? Does it mark a special moment in their life? Does it carry a story?

I grew up understanding clothes this way.

I come from a family where garments were passed down. Pieces didn't just disappear after one wear. They stayed, they evolved. I remember my mum giving me an aso-oke dress she had owned for years. Instead of leaving it as it was, I transformed it into a jumpsuit. It became something new, but it still carried its history with it.

That's value. That's what I think we're losing in fashion today.

Now everything feels fast. Disposable. One wear, one post, and it's gone. But clothing shouldn't be like that. It shouldn't be temporary.

And I understand. Someone might say, "Well, I can afford it. I can wear it once and move on." But I think there's a more important question we should be asking:

Where does the waste go?

It's a question a lot of people don't even stop to consider. When you throw something away, where does it actually end up? Because it doesn't just disappear.

We're living in a world, especially in parts of Africa, where the effects of waste are visible. Where systems are already strained. Where conversations around sustainability, waste, and even clean energy are becoming more urgent.

The small decisions we make, what we buy, how we wear it, how quickly we discard it, add up over time. You may not see the impact immediately, but in the long run, it's there.

So before it becomes "one wear, one post, and it's gone" simply because you can afford to replace it, pause and ask yourself: where does that waste go?

I aspire to create pieces that last. Pieces that people keep, love, and eventually pass down. Clothes that become heirlooms. Clothes that mean something long after the moment they were first worn.

I remember making a dress for a Namibian celebrity in 2018. Years later, when I went back to find photos of my work, I noticed something before I even got to that year. She had worn the dress again in 2020, or 2021.

And that meant everything to me. Because she didn't have to. She could have worn it once and moved on. But she didn't. The dress still held value for her.

That is the kind of fashion I believe in.

So no, we don't need more clothes. We need more thoughtful clothes. More intentional clothes. More sustainable clothes. Clothes that are made with care, for the wearer and for the world.

Because fashion affects more than just how we look. It affects our environment, our communities, and our future.

If you're a designer, I think this is something you should always ask yourself: what value am I creating? Not just for today, but for years to come.

Fashion, in its own way, can heal the world. But only if we choose to create differently.
